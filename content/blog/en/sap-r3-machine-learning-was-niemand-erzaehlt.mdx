When you watch a forecasting vendor's machine-learning demo, the dataset
looks like this: a clean timestamp, an SKU number, a quantity. Nicely
uniform, one data point per day. Maybe a few features like day-of-week or
season.

When you let that same vendor loose on your SAP R/3 data, things happen.
Here are the five most common — and what they mean.

## 1. Duplicate postings that aren't duplicates

Goods receipts in R/3 get posted multiple times: original posting,
correction, reversal, re-posting. If you naively aggregate by `BUDAT` you
count reversals as positive movement. Your forecast learns a seasonality
that's actually a posting routine.

**Consequence:** you need an ETL step that resolves reversal flags
(`SHKZG = 'H'` followed by `SHKZG = 'S'`) before you aggregate. No model in
the world compensates for this automatically.

## 2. Phantom SKUs

Material numbers get renumbered. Predecessor/successor relationships in R/3
are often kept only as free-text fields — when at all. From a forecasting
perspective this is fatal: your model sees 18 months of history, an abrupt
gap, then a "new" SKU with no history.

**Consequence:** you need a **mapping layer** that joins predecessors and
successors — either from an additional master-data system or from a manually
maintained mapping table. Cold-start strategies help, but only reliably if
the mapping is correct.

## 3. "Unit of measure"

In R/3 an SKU often has multiple units: piece, case, pallet. Anyone
aggregating in the wrong unit gets forecasts that are off by a factor of 12
or 24. This isn't a hypothetical — we've seen it in real pipelines.

**Consequence:** convert **everything to a base unit** (usually the smallest
one used for postings) and plan in that unit. Conversion to "order unit"
happens at the end of the pipeline, not the beginning.

## 4. Holidays aren't in the dataset

Sounds trivial, isn't. A weekly model sees a week with three holidays like
any other week — unless you've built that feature explicitly. The
consequence: the model learns that calendar week 52 is "always weak"
instead of understanding it's weak **because** of the holidays.

**Consequence:** a **holiday calendar** as a feature is mandatory.
State-specific in Germany — Bavaria has different holidays than Berlin.
External sources like [feiertage-api.de](https://feiertage-api.de) work,
but have to be wired into the ETL.

## 5. The "last day" isn't the last day

R/3 postings are recorded with delay. If you generate a forecast today
(Thursday), Wednesday's postings are often not complete yet. Train your
model assuming "last day = current day" and it learns a systematic negative
bias on the last two observations.

**Consequence:** define a **cutoff** — typically two days before generation
time. Forecast from there and accept that the last two days have to be
treated as a backfill window.

## The common pattern

All five points share one thing: **they're not an ML problem, they're a
data-integration problem.** They aren't solved by a better model. They're
solved by a clean ETL that knows R/3's quirks.

That's also why "bring us your CSV, we'll train a model" vendors fail so
often the moment they hit real mid-market data. The demo data is clean. R/3
isn't. Nobody is. And a model is only ever as good as the pipeline in front
of it.

## What we've learned

When you start a forecasting project with SAP R/3, plan **two thirds of the
time for data work**, not for model training. Anyone promising you
otherwise has either never worked with R/3 or is in the process of
learning — at your expense.

That's not a tragedy. It's the reality of every ML project that touches
real business data. But it's a reality that rarely gets discussed in the
glossy brochures. We prefer to discuss it up front.
