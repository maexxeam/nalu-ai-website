ABC/XYZ is the technique every supply-chain manager learned in school. A-items
are the important ones, C-items are the unimportant ones, X-items are
predictable, Z-items are chaotic. Sounds simple. It is — until you try to
actually base decisions on it.

## What ABC/XYZ really is

ABC is a **Pareto classification by contribution**. Usually by revenue or
margin. The logic: 20% of SKUs make up 80% of value.

XYZ is a **variability classification**. It measures how steady demand is over
time — typically via the coefficient of variation (standard deviation / mean).

Combined they yield a 3×3 matrix:

|     | X (predictable) | Y (medium) | Z (volatile) |
|-----|-----------------|------------|--------------|
| **A** | AX — standard | AY — careful | AZ — worth the effort |
| **B** | BX | BY | BZ |
| **C** | CX | CY | CZ — automate |

So far so good.

## Where it starts to hurt

Three misunderstandings come up over and over:

**1. "A-item = important, C-item = unimportant."**
Wrong. A C-item can be critical when it completes an A-item (think: a spare
part without which the machine doesn't run). ABC measures **value**, not
**importance**.

**2. "Z-items can't be forecast."**
Wrong. Z-items are volatile — but volatile doesn't mean random. Often the
volatility is structural (seasonal promo, B2B bulk orders). With confidence
intervals instead of point forecasts, even a Z-item becomes plannable — just
not reducible to a single number.

**3. "The matrix is forever."**
Wrong. The classification is a snapshot. We regularly see SKUs migrating from
CZ to AY (or back) within twelve months. **ABC/XYZ has to run continuously,
not be rebuilt once a year in a spreadsheet.**

## What you should derive from ABC/XYZ

If the classification runs correctly, the implications are clear:

- **AX items** are the foundation. Precise weekly forecasting and tight safety
  stock pay off here.
- **AZ items** are the most expensive bad calls. They need confidence
  intervals, scenario planning and a human sanity check.
- **CZ items** should be automated. Reorder points based on service level,
  fixed reorder cycles, no forecasting effort.

## What you should not derive from ABC/XYZ

Strategic assortment decisions. Supplier consolidation. Make-or-buy. Those are
topics where ABC/XYZ is an input signal but never the decision.

## The underrated variant: ABC by margin

Most companies run ABC by revenue. That's good for sales KPIs, dangerous for
supply chain. A high-revenue, low-margin SKU ties up capital without
contributing proportionally to profitability. **ABC by contribution margin**
often paints a different picture — and leads to smarter inventory decisions.

## If you were starting today

Three pragmatic steps:

1. Run ABC by revenue **and** ABC by contribution margin — and analyze the
   gap.
2. Calculate XYZ over the last 12 months **and** over the last 3. Structural
   shifts become visible.
3. Refresh the matrix monthly. Manual = effort. Automated = a configuration
   question.

ABC/XYZ isn't an end in itself. It's a tool that tells you **where to invest
forecasting effort** — and where you can skip it. That's what it's good for.
You shouldn't expect more. But that's already plenty.
