There's a moment in nearly every mid-market company when somebody says, "We do
that in Excel." Nobody decided. It just happened. Ten years ago somebody
needed a quick overview — and ever since, the spreadsheet has wandered from
desk to desk, grown, branched. Today, the order quantity for next week hangs
on a VLOOKUP formula nobody understands end-to-end anymore.

## Excel isn't the problem. The pipeline is.

Excel sheets for forecasting aren't wrong because Excel is wrong. Excel is an
excellent tool. They're wrong because **the pipeline around them is missing**:

- Who refreshes the data? When? From which system?
- What happens when a goods receipt is posted at 23:30?
- What happens when an SKU was renumbered two weeks ago?
- What happens when the person with the formulas is on vacation?

In an ML pipeline, all of those are **defined steps**. In Excel they're
**implicit expectations** — and expectations that aren't written down get
violated eventually.

## Three symptoms everyone knows

**1. Forecasts go stale in hours.**
You generate order proposals Sunday night. Monday morning a goods-receipt
correction comes in. The proposals are wrong now — but nobody notices,
because the spreadsheet has no idea it's stale.

**2. Cold starts are gambling.**
A new SKU has no history. The Excel logic either falls back to a hard-coded
default or returns `#N/A`. Both mean: you're not planning, you're guessing.

**3. Nobody knows why the forecast looks the way it does.**
If the model says "next week 480 units" — on what basis? Seasonality? Trend?
Marketing campaign? In Excel: no answer. At best: a comment from a colleague
in 2021.

## What a real pipeline does differently

A modern forecasting pipeline doesn't solve these symptoms with a "better
Excel," but with three properties Excel structurally lacks:

1. **Reproducibility.** Every forecast is generated by a defined model on a
   defined data state. If I rerun last week's forecast today, I get the same
   answer.
2. **Explainability.** Every prediction comes with a confidence interval and
   — when needed — with SHAP values showing which features drove the value.
3. **Resilience.** When a feature is missing, the model falls back to a
   defined strategy. It doesn't go silent and it doesn't lie.

## "But Excel works for us."

The answer isn't "no, it doesn't." The answer is: **Excel works exactly up
until the moment it gets expensive that it doesn't.** Up to that point,
everything is quiet. The day the first major order gets cancelled because the
warehouse is empty looks like any other day — until 2 p.m.

If you're at the point where Excel is the control room of your supply chain,
the question isn't **whether** to switch but **when**. Ideally before the
expensive day.

## The middle path no one advertises

The industry offers two extremes: the Excel status quo and the SAP IBP
implementation for €250,000 + consulting. What's missing in mid-market is what
technical teams have known from open source for years: a deterministic,
config-driven pipeline that runs in weeks, not quarters.

That's what Nalu AI exists for. But more importantly:
**you're allowed to have expectations.** Forecasts don't have to be mystical.
They have to be explainable. And they shouldn't live in a spreadsheet that a
single sick day brings down.
